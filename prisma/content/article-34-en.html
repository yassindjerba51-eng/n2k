In the food industry, productivity is closely linked to equipment availability. However, during audits of cleaning organisation, a recurring operational finding emerges: hygiene crews spend a disproportionate amount of time in intermediate or final rinsing phases. These prolonged rinsing times reduce available production time and unnecessarily increase the plant's overall water consumption.

Technical causes of extended rinsing phases

The need to rinse installations abundantly often stems from unsuitable chemical protocols or application methods:

  • Excessive foam on equipment: Using uncalibrated or overdosed foaming products generates dense and voluminous foam. This foam penetrates machine corners and proves extremely difficult to flush away, requiring massive rinse water volumes for total removal.
  • Persistence of surfactant residues: Some classic detergents leave a sticky chemical film on stainless steel. Operators must rinse repeatedly to eliminate this film and avoid chemical contamination of subsequent food products.
  • Inaccessibility of complex zones: Rinsing articulated conveyors or dosing heads requires complex and repeated actions to ensure that no residues of classic detergents or foaming disinfectants remain trapped.

Consequences on industrial organisation

These repeated delays in the rinsing phase push back production startup times, increasing stress on crews and the risk of shortening disinfectant contact times to compensate. Additionally, massive warm rinse water input increases ambient relative humidity and condensation in cold workshops, promoting subsequent microbiological development risks.
