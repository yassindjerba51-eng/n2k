In many food processing workshops, hygiene control appears flawless during checks carried out after the main daily cleaning. However, as soon as machines run and production shifts progress, a worrying phenomenon occurs: food contact surfaces rapidly become recontaminated between two production runs. This biological load transfer, often invisible to the naked eye, is a major critical point for the sanitary safety of finished products.

Vectors of rapid recontamination during production

The industrial environment is dynamic. Even after rigorous disinfection, several factors promote the rapid resettlement of micro-organisms on production tools during operations:

  • Continuous organic projections: Cutting, conveying, and packaging release micro-droplets of juice, fat, or organic dust. These residues settle on surfaces and form an immediately available nutrient source for bacteria.
  • Mechanical friction and contact transfers: Raw materials introduce their own microbiological flora. Upon contact with conveyor belts or cutting blades, these bacteria are transferred and accumulate over the hours.
  • Human factors and handling: Rapid interventions by operators to adjust a machine or unblock a product introduce cross-contamination, particularly on handles, control buttons, and table edges.

Why classic cleaning is impractical mid-shift

Between two manufacturing runs or during recipe changes, time windows are extremely short, often lasting only a few minutes. It is technically impossible to deploy a complete protocol of washing with large amounts of water and foam disinfection, which would require hours of downtime and generate excessive relative humidity in cold rooms.
